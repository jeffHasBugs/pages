Sum and product of roots

23/03/2022

In this post I tried to come up with an intuitive explanation for why, given the quadratic equation ax^2 + bx + c = 0, the sum of the roots of the equation is -b/a, and the product of the roots is c/a.

I spent more time than was necessary in attempting to write a clear explanation for my thought processes. The explanation is not clear, nor concise, but I'm posting it here anyways because I'm quite proud that I could even come up with an explanation.

Why is the sum of two roots of a quadratic equation (-b/a) and the product (c/a)?

a. Example x^2 - 18x + 81=0 ; RootA+RootB = -(-18/1) , RootA*RootB = 81/1 ;
b. Example 5x^2 - 9x + 4=0 ; RootA+RootB = -(-9/5) , RootA*RootB = 4/5 ;

Observing from the examples: In a, the equation can be simplified (x-9)(x-9) = 0 , where x=9. We observe that the coefficient of x is simply the sum of the roots.

But wait! In b, the equation can be simplified (5x - 4)(x - 1) = 0, where x = 4/5, but the coefficient of x is -9. It now seems that the coefficient of x is not just the sum of constants in the simplified equation.

Explanation

For any equation (mx - c)(nx - d) = 0, x= c/m or x= d/n. The coefficient of x^2 is the product of the coefficients of x in the simplified equation. The coefficient of x in the expanded quadratic equation is cn + dm. Taking cn/nm + dm/nm, we obtain c/m + d/n, or the sum of the roots, b/a!

Why negate b/a (multiply b/a with -1)? When (yx-a = 0), x=a/y , but the coefficient of x is derived from -a. In other words, the sign of true root of the quadratic equation is almost always opposite to the sign of a.

For (mx - c)(nx + d) = 0, the coefficient of x^0 in the quadratic equation is (-c*d). The roots c/m and -d/n. The same principles apply to c/a. Taking the coefficient of x^0, (-c*d) / mn = -c*d/m/n.

This multiplication of roots turns negative if the constants have different signs. It does not discriminate between the individual sign of constants c and d. Negation is not needed.
