Sum and product of roots

07/04/2022

In this post I tried to come up with an intuitive explanation for why, given the quadratic equation ax^2 + bx + c = 0, the sum of the roots of the equation is -b/a, and the product of the roots is c/a.

I spent more time than was necessary in attempting to write a clear explanation for my thought processes. The explanation is not clear, nor concise, but I'm posting it here anyways.

Why is the sum of two roots of a quadratic equation (-b/a) and the product (c/a)?

a. x^2 - 18x + 81=0 ; Root1+Root2 = -(-18/1) , Root1*Root2 = 81/1 ;
b. 5x^2 - 9x + 4=0 ; Root1+Root2 = -(-9/5) , Root1*Root2 = 4/5 ;


For any equation (mx - c)(nx - d) = 0 The roots of the quadratic equation are x= c/m or x= d/n. The coefficient of x^2 in the quadratic equation is the product of the coefficients of x in the simplified equation. The coefficient of x in the expanded quadratic equation is cn + dm. Taking cn/nm + dm/nm, we obtain c/m + d/n, or the sum of the roots, b/a!

Why multiply b/a with -1? When mx-c = 0, x=c/m , but the coefficient of x in the quadratic equation (the b in b/a) is derived from -c. In this case, b/a is the sum of -c/m and the other root (real root is positive). To correct for the negative c, we negate the number. Combining all these findings, we get -b/a.

What about c/a? For (mx - c)(nx + d) = 0, the roots are c/m and -d/n. The coefficient of x^0 (c in c/a) in the expanded quadratic equation is (-c*d). The roots are c/m and -d/n. Take (-c*d) / mn = -c/m * d/n, we get the product of the roots. Note that it doesn't matter if which root (c and d in this case) is negative or positive - if they have exclusively different signs, the product will be negative.
